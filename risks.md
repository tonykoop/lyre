# Risks

## Primary Risks

### Historical silhouette drift

Torii language, ergonomic shaping, strap anchors, and pickup accommodation can
push the design away from the ancient Greek read if they become too literal or
visually loud.

Mitigation: keep the front silhouette conservative and move bolder gestures to
the stand, back, side, or removable accessories.

### Structural overload at arms and yoke

Thirteen strings plus optional strap loads can over-stress the arm sockets,
yoke saddles, and shoulder blocks.

Mitigation: run tension studies early, prototype the mule first, and treat
strap support as secondary until the structure is validated.

### Ergonomic feature choking the voice

A lap saddle, pad, or aggressive rear-hand relief could reduce resonance or
create local weakness.

Mitigation: A/B acoustic tests with removable interfaces before final carving.

### Decorative mass in active acoustic zones

Broinwood-style layered ornament can become acoustically expensive if placed in
the wrong area.

Mitigation: keep the soundboard decoration sparse and shift most deep work to
the stand, side, back, peg heads, and yoke details.

### Pickup path becoming irreversible too early

It would be easy to over-commit to electronics before the acoustic instrument
is proven.

Mitigation: start with reversible contact-transducer experiments and postpone
hard-mounted jacks until the acoustic version is accepted.

## Open Risk Retirements

- Build full-scale ergonomic mockups.
- Produce a first-pass tension table.
- Test temporary strap anchors on the mule.
- Make small inlay sample boards before cutting the final top.
